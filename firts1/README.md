# Parametric Study of Photovoltaic–Thermoelectric Generator with Passive Thermal Management

## Overview

The integration of photovoltaic (PV) systems with thermoelectric generators (TEGs) presents a promising approach to enhancing the overall efficiency of solar energy systems. Traditional PV systems convert only a portion of the incident solar energy into electrical power, while a significant amount of energy is dissipated as heat. This heat accumulation increases the operating temperature of the PV module, which negatively affects its electrical efficiency.

Thermoelectric generators (TEGs) utilize the Seebeck effect to convert a temperature difference across thermoelectric materials into electrical energy. In a hybrid PV–TE system, the TEG is placed at the backside of the PV module to harvest a portion of the thermal energy that would otherwise be lost.

This project presents a parametric study of a hybrid PV–TE system using MATLAB simulations to evaluate how the temperature difference across the thermoelectric generator influences the efficiency and power output of the hybrid system.

---

# Hybrid PV–TE System Configuration

![Hybrid PV-TE System](../figures/PV%20TEG%20System.png)

The PV–TE hybrid system consists of a photovoltaic cell and a thermoelectric generator. The PV cell converts part of the solar radiation into electricity, while the remaining energy is transformed into heat, leading to an increase in the PV cell temperature.

The hot side of the thermoelectric generator absorbs this heat flux and converts a portion of it into electrical energy, while the rest of the heat is transferred to the cold side of the thermoelectric generator.

---

# Energy Balance of the PV–TE Hybrid System

![Energy Balance](../figures/energy_balance.png)

The energy balance in the PV cell includes the incident solar radiation, electrical power generated by the PV module, and heat losses through conduction, convection, and radiation.

The conductive heat loss from the PV cell is absorbed by the thermoelectric generator, which converts part of this thermal energy into additional electrical power.

---

# Efficiency of the PV–TE Hybrid System

![Efficiency Graph](../figures/efficiency_plot.png)

This figure illustrates the efficiency of the generic PV–TE hybrid system, the PV cell, and the thermoelectric generator as a function of the temperature difference across the thermoelectric generator.

The PV efficiency decreases with increasing temperature difference due to thermal degradation, while the efficiency of the thermoelectric generator increases with the temperature gradient.

The hybrid system efficiency represents the combined contribution of the photovoltaic module and the thermoelectric generator.

# Performance of Different Photovoltaic Materials

The hybrid PV–TE system was analyzed for three different photovoltaic materials:

• Amorphous Silicon (a-Si)
• Monocrystalline Silicon (Mono-Si)
• Cadmium Telluride (CdTe)

These materials exhibit different electrical efficiencies and temperature coefficients, which influence the overall performance of the hybrid system.

## Monocrystalline Silicon (Mono-Si) PV–TE Hybrid System

![Mono-Si PV-TE System](Mono-Si.png)

The monocrystalline silicon photovoltaic module provides higher baseline efficiency compared to amorphous silicon. However, its efficiency decreases more rapidly with increasing temperature.

The integration of a thermoelectric generator allows part of the thermal energy to be converted into additional electrical power.

## Cadmium Telluride (CdTe) PV–TE Hybrid System

![CdTe PV-TE System](CdTe.png)

Cadmium telluride photovoltaic modules offer moderate efficiency and improved thermal stability. The hybrid PV–TE configuration demonstrates the combined effect of photovoltaic efficiency degradation and thermoelectric energy generation.

# MATLAB Simulation

The performance of the hybrid PV–TE system was evaluated using MATLAB simulations. The model incorporates the temperature dependence of photovoltaic efficiency and the thermoelectric conversion characteristics of the thermoelectric generator.

The simulation calculates:

• Photovoltaic efficiency
• Thermoelectric efficiency
• Hybrid system efficiency
• Hybrid power output

as a function of the temperature difference across the thermoelectric generator.

---

# Key Observations

• Photovoltaic efficiency decreases as the temperature difference increases.
• Thermoelectric generator efficiency increases with increasing temperature gradient.
• The hybrid PV–TE system improves the overall energy utilization by converting part of the waste heat into electrical power.
• The performance of the hybrid system depends strongly on the photovoltaic material used.
